Our History

Our journey began at the Cypress Forest Recreation Center, where we met by chance in response to a new belly dance class. This class was led by our wonderful matriarch instructor, Angela, who shared her immense skill, her love for dance, and the beautiful choreography that we still practice and perform to this day. Angela’s belly dance style was deeply influenced by her Greek heritage—a culture she shared with us through both dance and language. Her background, ranging from childhood dance to collegiate training and professional performance, gave us a rich foundation that we carry with us even now.

In those early days, many of us had little experience with dance. Belly dance gave us the opportunity to discover the world of movement for the first time, while others in the class brought experience from backgrounds like ballet, ballroom, and various Middle Eastern and Egyptian styles. In addition, we were given the opportunity to dance in the community at local Haflas and other events, which truly opened our world; these collaborations offered us new perspectives that complemented our own experiences with dance.

The Naming of Leonard's Ladies

Eventually, our initial group of eclectic dancers—including our teacher and steadfast members Janet and Jackie, who were there from the very beginning—decided on a name. They chose Leonard’s Ladies, in honor of the recreation center’s coach and manager, Leonard. We credit Janet for ultimately choosing the name that has endured to this day.

Memories on Stage

Our performance memories are as diverse as our group. Janet and Jackie hold the "shiny leggings" memories from their very first Hafla performances, while my own early stage experience began with the troupe’s vibrant Country Fusion routine—complete with bell bottoms, fringes, and cowboy hats! Whether it was those initial nervous moments in early costumes or the fun of later performances, we look back at every step with nostalgia and pride, reflecting on how far our skills and our costumes have come since those first shows.

Growth and Evolution

Angela led the class for several years, teaching us many more diverse styles of belly dance and fusion, including elements of Folk, Hip-Hop, Zumba, Bollywood style, soft rock, and drum solos. She also taught us to master props like veils, fan veils, chairs, zills, and Isis wings. This gave us the opportunity to participate in many live performances, ranging from local restaurants and country clubs to bayside park venues. A major milestone for Angela—which we were all proud to share in—occurred when we performed at St. Pete’s Acropolis Theater, joining other local belly dance troupes to open for the Belly Dance Superstars while they were on tour, dancing to Angela’s original choreography for "Voulez-Vous." It wasn’t long after that Angela transitioned from the group to pursue new endeavors in fitness, wellness, and family.

Interim Leadership

During that transition, we relied on interim instructors until Valerie returned to share even more styles, including Samba, Hip-Hop fusions, and props like Tahitian i'i (hand tassels) and fire performance—a skill mastered by Jackie and Janet. She also taught us original choreography that we treasure and still practice to this day. In fact, Val is the one who taught us that fun Country Fusion number as well as that silly funny hip move.

A New Chapter

Ultimately, Janet saved the day by stepping in as our current instructor to bring our class back together and honor the foundation laid by our mentor. To mark the occasion, she debuted her first original choreography to "Santa Baby," introducing us to the boa as a prop. Janet also led the class in learning the memorable Thrill the World choreography to Michael Jackson’s "Thriller," and introduced us to new forms of movement including SharQui (Arabic for "Eastern" dance), Hula-Hooping, and the Essence of Bellydance method. Her journey is particularly inspiring; even after a life-altering car accident, she used belly dance as a vital part of her healing process, eventually regaining the strength to stand, walk, and return to the stage. Today, we continue to grow, perform, and support one another, proving that our bond is as resilient as the dance itself. She remains our treasured Leonard’s Ladies instructor until this very day.
